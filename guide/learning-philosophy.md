# Learning Philosophy

> **Understanding begins with curiosity, not answers.**

The purpose of this project is not to memorize how Large Language Models work, but to rediscover the ideas that made them possible.

Every technology is the result of someone solving a problem. To truly understand a technology, we must first understand the problem that inspired it. Rather than starting with finished solutions, this project encourages you to think like an engineer and a researcher—asking questions, forming hypotheses, building solutions, and learning through experimentation.

![Learning Philosophy](../assets/learning_philosophy.png)

Our goal is simple:

> **Don't just learn AI. Learn how to think about AI.**

---

# The Learning Framework

Every topic in this project follows the same learning framework.

```text
Question
    ↓
Problem
    ↓
Hypothesis
    ↓
Theory
    ↓
Implementation
    ↓
Experiment
    ↓
Reflection
```

This framework is designed to transform passive learning into active discovery.

---

# 1. Question

Every meaningful discovery begins with a question.

Instead of immediately searching for answers, begin by identifying what you genuinely want to understand.

Examples:

* How does GPT predict the next token?
* Why does Attention exist?
* Why wasn't an RNN enough?
* Why do we need embeddings?

A good question creates direction for learning.

---

# 2. Problem

Engineering begins with problems, not solutions.

Before studying a concept, understand the problem it was created to solve.

Ask yourself:

* What limitation existed?
* Why was a new solution necessary?
* What would happen if this problem remained unsolved?

When the problem is clear, the solution often feels inevitable.

---

# 3. Hypothesis

Before reading the official explanation, create your own.

Predict how you think the problem could be solved.

Your hypothesis does **not** need to be correct.

Its purpose is to develop independent thinking.

Comparing your own reasoning with established knowledge builds much stronger intuition than simply reading the answer.

---

# 4. Theory

Now learn the current solution.

Study how the idea actually works and, more importantly, why it works.

This is where mathematics, algorithms, and engineering principles come together.

The objective is not to memorize formulas but to understand the reasoning behind them.

---

# 5. Implementation

Understanding becomes much deeper when ideas become code.

Whenever possible, implement concepts from scratch before relying on high-level libraries.

Building reveals details that reading often hides.

Implementation transforms theoretical knowledge into practical understanding.

---

# 6. Experiment

Do not stop once something works.

Change it.

Break it.

Replace parts.

Observe what happens.

Questions such as these often lead to the deepest insights:

* What happens if this component is removed?
* What happens if the learning rate becomes extremely large?
* What happens if embeddings have only two dimensions?
* What happens if Attention is replaced with averaging?

Experiments build intuition through observation rather than memorization.

---

# 7. Reflection

Learning does not end after implementation.

Pause and think.

Ask yourself:

* What did I actually understand?
* Which assumptions were incorrect?
* What limitations still exist?
* Could I explain this idea without looking at notes?
* If I had to redesign it today, what would I change?

Reflection transforms information into lasting knowledge.

---

# Why This Framework?

Most learning follows a simple pattern:

```text
Read
    ↓
Memorize
    ↓
Forget
```

This project follows a different approach:

```text
Question
    ↓
Understand
    ↓
Build
    ↓
Experiment
    ↓
Reflect
```

The objective is not to collect information.

The objective is to develop intuition.

---

# First Principles Thinking

Every chapter begins with the same assumption:

> **Every engineering solution exists because a previous solution was not enough.**

Rather than asking:

> *How does this work?*

We first ask:

> *Why does this exist?*

Understanding the "why" makes the "how" much easier to understand.

---

# Learning Through Building

Reading creates knowledge.

Building creates understanding.

Experimentation creates intuition.

Reflection creates wisdom.

For this reason, every major concept in this project is accompanied by implementation and experimentation.

You are encouraged to build every important component yourself—from a tokenizer and embedding layer to a complete Transformer and AI assistant.

---

# Learning Through Failure

Failure is not a mistake in this project—it is part of the learning process.

Incorrect hypotheses, failed implementations, and unexpected experimental results often produce the strongest understanding.

Never be afraid to be wrong.

Be afraid of accepting an answer without understanding it.

---

# Think Like an Engineer

Every chapter should leave you asking questions such as:

* Why was this design chosen?
* What trade-offs does it make?
* What would happen if I removed it?
* How would I redesign it?

The goal is not simply to reproduce existing systems but to develop the ability to reason about future ones.

---

# The Ultimate Goal

By the end of this journey, success is not measured by how many terms you remember.

Success is measured by whether you can:

* Understand a new AI paper without feeling overwhelmed.
* Explain why an architecture exists.
* Build the core components yourself.
* Identify limitations in existing approaches.
* Form evidence-based hypotheses about new ideas.
* Continue learning independently as the field evolves.

Technology changes.

Frameworks change.

Models change.

**First principles do not.**

That is the philosophy behind this project.
